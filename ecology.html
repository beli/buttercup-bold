Ecology

The Western Ghats, stretching along the western edge of peninsular India, form one of the most ecologically significant regions in the world. Running parallel to the Arabian Sea for over 1,600 kilometers, this mountain chain acts as both a climatic barrier and a biodiversity hotspot, shaping the environment of much of southern India.

Ecologically, the Western Ghats are remarkable for their extraordinary biodiversity and endemism. The range is recognized as one of the world’s eight “hottest” biodiversity hotspots, meaning it contains a high number of species found nowhere else, while also facing significant threats. Dense tropical evergreen forests dominate the windward slopes, nourished by heavy monsoon rainfall. These forests transition into moist deciduous and then dry deciduous forests as rainfall decreases eastward, creating a mosaic of habitats within a relatively narrow geographical band.
